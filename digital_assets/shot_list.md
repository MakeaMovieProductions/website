# Shot Listing 101

### Recommended Shot List Structure
To make it functional for a shoot, set up your columns with these headers:

1. Shot #: Sequential number (e.g., 1, 2, 3).

2. Scene #: Corresponding scene from the script.

3. Status: Use Data Validation to create a dropdown menu with options like "To Shoot," "In Progress," and "Wrapped."

4. Description: A brief note on the action (e.g., "CU of Protagonist looking at watch").

5. Location: Where the shot takes place.

6. Equipment/Lens: Specific gear needed for that shot.

7. Checkmark: A column with checkboxes (Insert > Checkbox) for quick ticking on set.

Add the following columns which have a drop down button to choose for each of these columns

1. Shot Sizes (The "Scale")These describe how much of the character’s body or the environment is in the frame.

- **Extreme Wide Shot (EWS)**: Shows the landscape; the character is tiny or not visible. Used as an Establishing Shot.
- **Wide Shot (WS) / Long Shot (LS)**: The character's entire body is visible from head to toe, with significant background.
- **Full Shot (FS)**: Frames the character from head to toe, filling most of the height of the frame.
- **Medium Wide / Medium Long Shot (MWS/MLS)**: Also called the "Cowboy Shot," it cuts the character off at the mid-thigh (originally to show a gunslinger's holsters).
- **Medium Shot (MS)**: One of the most common; frames the character from the waist up.
- **Medium Close-Up (MCU)**: Frames from the chest up.
- **Close-Up (CU)**: Fills the screen with the character’s face (shoulder line up). Essential for emotion.
- **Extreme Close-Up (ECU)**: Focuses on a single detail—an eye, a mouth, or a ticking watch.

2. Camera Angles (The "Perspective")
These dictate how the audience feels about the character (e.g., powerful or vulnerable).

- **Eye Level**: The standard "neutral" shot.
- **Low Angle**: Camera looks up at the subject, making them look powerful, heroic, or intimidating.
- **High Angle**: Camera looks down on the subject, making them look small, weak, or vulnerable.
- **Bird’s Eye (Overhead)**: Shot from directly above.
- **Worm’s Eye**: Shot from ground level looking straight up.
- **Dutch Angle**: The camera is tilted to one side. This creates a feeling of unease, madness, or disorientation.

3. Framing & Composition (The "Relationship")
These define how many people are in the frame or how they interact.

- **Single**: One person in frame.
- **Two-Shot / Three-Shot**: Two or three people in the frame.
- **Over-the-Shoulder (OTS)**: Looking over one character's shoulder at another. Common in dialogue.
- **Point of View (POV)**: The camera "becomes" the character's eyes.
- **Insert Shot**: A shot of a specific object (like a ringing phone) that is edited into a scene

4. Camera Movement (The "Energy")
Movement adds kinetic energy and helps tell the story.

- **Pan**: The camera rotates horizontally on a fixed axis (left to right).
- **Tilt**: The camera rotates vertically on a fixed axis (up and down).
- **Dolly**: The entire camera moves forward or backward on a track.
- **Tracking Shot**: The camera moves alongside the subject as they move.
- **Crane / Jib Shot**: The camera moves vertically through the air.
- **Handheld**: Creates a gritty, "real-life" or shaky feeling.
- **Steadicam / Gimbal**: Smooth, floating movement.
